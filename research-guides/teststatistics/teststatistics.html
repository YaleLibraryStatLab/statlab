Abstract

This guide provides an overview of test statistics—quantitative measures used to decide whether to reject or fail to reject a null hypothesis. It introduces the conceptual foundation of hypothesis testing, explains the mathematical intuition behind common test statistics such as the z, t, F, and chi-square, and demonstrates their implementation in R, Python, and Stata.

Keywords

Introduction

Welcome to this research guide on Test Statistics!

Test statistics quantify the relationship between sample data and a null hypothesis. They serve as the bridge between descriptive data summaries and inferential decisions.

  • This guide outlines the conceptual and mathematical foundations of key test statistics.
  • It also demonstrates how to compute and interpret them using statistical software.

Methods

Theoretical Intuition

In hypothesis testing, we compare observed data with what we would expect if the null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) were true. The test statistic measures how far the observed sample statistic deviates from the expected value under \(H_0\), scaled by its standard error.

A large absolute value of the test statistic indicates stronger evidence against \(H_0\).

Mathematical Basis

Most test statistics follow a general form:

\[ \text{Test Statistic} = \frac{\text{Estimate} - \text{Null Value}}{\text{Standard Error}} \]

Depending on assumptions about the population variance and sample size, this yields different distributions:

Test Type Formula Assumed Distribution
Z-test \(z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}\) Standard Normal
t-test \(t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}\) Student’s t
F-test \(F = \frac{s_1^2 / \nu_1}{s_2^2 / \nu_2}\) F distribution
χ²-test \(\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}\) Chi-square

Mathematically, \(z\) and \(t\) statistics both quantify standardized deviations, but differ in whether population variance is known.

Implementation

1. Computing Test Statistics

Test statistics can be implemented directly from formulas or through built-in functions in most software packages.

2. Comparing to Critical Values

Each statistic is compared to a critical value from its corresponding theoretical distribution, or converted to a p-value to determine statistical significance.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)
sample_data = np.random.normal(5.2, 1, 30)
t_stat, p_val = stats.ttest_1samp(sample_data, popmean=5)

Interpretation

Interpretation depends on the context and directionality of the hypothesis:

  • If the p-value < α (e.g., 0.05), reject the null hypothesis.
  • The sign of the statistic indicates the direction of deviation.
  • Magnitude reflects how extreme the observed data are under \(H_0\).

Example: A t-statistic of 2.45 with df = 29 corresponds to p ≈ 0.02, suggesting the sample mean differs significantly from the hypothesized population mean at the 5% level.

FAQs

When should I use a t-test instead of a z-test?

Use a t-test when the population standard deviation is unknown and must be estimated from the sample. The z-test assumes known population variance, which is rare in practice.

What’s the difference between F and t tests?

An F-test compares variances or multiple means (e.g., ANOVA), whereas a t-test compares two means. In fact, for two groups, \(t^2 = F\).

Why do we use chi-square tests for categorical data?

Because categorical outcomes are counts or frequencies, the chi-square test measures how observed frequencies differ from expected frequencies under \(H_0\).
